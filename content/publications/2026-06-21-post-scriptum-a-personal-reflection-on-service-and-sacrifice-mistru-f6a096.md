**Post-Scriptum: A Personal Reflection on Service and Sacrifice**

In researching and drafting this treatise, I found myself returning again and again to **Sophocles’ *Ajax***—a play that grapples with honour, betrayal, and the meaning of a life well-lived. Ajax, the great warrior, is denied the armour of Achilles, not because he is unworthy, but because the Greek commanders favour Odysseus’ cunning over his brute strength. In his rage and despair, Ajax turns his sword upon himself, believing that a life without honour is no life at all.

The tragedy of Ajax is not that he dies, but that he **dies believing he has nothing left to give**. This is the fate we risk for a generation raised to believe that **consumption is purpose, that comfort is virtue, and that duty is oppression**.

Yet, there is another path.

In **Victor Hugo’s *Les Misérables***, Jean Valjean is not redeemed by wealth or power, but by **an act of mercy**—his decision to spare Javert, the man who has hunted him for decades. In that moment, Valjean chooses **service over self-interest**, and in doing so, he becomes more than a criminal, more than a fugitive. He becomes **a man**.

This is the promise of National Service. It is not about **subjugation**—it is about **transformation**. It is the chance for every citizen to say, as Valjean does: *"I am more than what the world has made me."*

To those who would dismiss this proposal as **utopian**, I say: **What is more utopian—believing that a society can survive without duty, or believing that it can be reborn through it?**

To those who would call it **coercive**, I say: **What is more coercive—asking a year of service from every citizen, or condemning millions to a lifetime of precarity and despair?**

And to those who would ask, *"Why now?"*—I answer with the words of **Aeschylus in *Prometheus Bound***:

> *"Look on these sufferings, and know that I am wiser than the wise."*

The time for half-measures is past. The time for **a new covenant** is now.
